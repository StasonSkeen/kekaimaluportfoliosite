order of ‘io

Revitalizing an ancient Polynesian belief system as a modern day cult

  • brand identity

    To understand the direction my design process would need to go I began research and understanding the religion's history, culture, and what kind of cult this would be. I discovered what kind of design, copywriting, textures, copywriting, etc that would best convey visually the purpose and message of the Order of ‘Io.

  • religious symbol

    The first thing I began designing was the religious symbol that would represent the Order of ‘Io, this symbol would be the Cross, Star of David, or Wheel of Dharma to the Order of ‘Io. From my research Anthropologist Dr. Daniel Kikawa cites Calvin Kawailani Eaton's collections of Hawaiian oral traditions that describes the puloʻuloʻu as a representation of the Supreme Being ‘Io. The puloʻuloʻu, a white kapa cloth ball wrapped atop a stick, was the symbol I used to represent ‘Io. Māori historian, Te Rangi Hiroa, also discovered that in Aotearoa an account of the cosmogony of the world described ‘Io as having come from "Te Kore" or the void, so I designed two logos a "positive" symbol (left) with ‘Io in a created world and a "negative" symbol (right) with ‘Io in Te Kore.

  • medium of creation

    Next I began designing posters that would be used to inform beleivers of the many descriptor names of ‘Io as cited by Te Rangi Hiroa. I wanted the design of the posters to feel "rough" like it were made in ancient times but to also be modern in some ways. To properly get this aesthetic I found Hala seeds from the floor that fell from a Hala tree and used this as a paint brush to design the representations of the names of ‘Io, these seed brushes provided the "roughness" I was looking for and scanning my paintings into a computer and designing allowed for the "moderness" to come through as well.

  • posters

    The posters correspond with 7 of the 27 descriptor names as cited by Te Rangi Hiroa translated in both ‘ōlelo Hawai‘i and te reo Māori with excerpts from differnet oral traditions that correspond with the names. ‘Io Mau (Io Roa) meaning ‘Io is eternal, ‘Io Makua ke ‘Ohe (Io Matua te Kore) meaning ‘Io was uncreated, ‘Io Makua (Io Matua), ‘Io is the parent of all things, ‘Io Maka Nalo (Io Mata Ngaro) ‘Io is the unseen, ‘Io Na‘auao (Io Wananga) ‘Io is the source of knowledge, ‘Io Nui (Io Nui) ‘Io is supreme, and ‘Io Waiola (Io Waiora) ‘Io is the source of life.

  • t-shirt

    Continuing design my designs aimed at beleivers of the faith of ‘Io, I created a T-shirt design that relates to the Order of ‘Io belief in the Huinakolu Lōkahi (Triangle of Harmony). The design features a Hawaiian hawk a representation of the Being ‘Io, a kapa pattern of the weaving of ti leaves, this kapa pattern represents a piko specifically the umblical chord showing the connection ‘Io has with all things created by ‘Io. The Hawaiian hawk is soaring in front of the Huinakolu Lōkahi members of the Order of ‘Io beleive that if these three things are kept in harmony life will be good ‘āina, kānaka, and ‘Io.

  • aloha shirt

    In the 1931 issue of the "Paradise of the Pacific" magazine, native Hawaiian historian Emma Anuhea Taylor described the history and teachings of the ‘Io beleivers in her article "The Cult of ‘Iolani." She described the clothing of ‘Io priests being the color yellow so to reflect the beliefs of the ancient faith I chose the colour yellow for the Aloha shirt. Another topic covered in Taylor's article was another name by which ‘Io went by which was Kue-manu-‘ai-lehua or the beak that feeds on "lehuas" or the power of death. To reflect this name I once again used the piko to represent ‘Io's power over life and the lehua as ‘Io's power over death.
